As a kid with nothing better to do, I would often watch my Dad for hours trying to fix broken electronics that family members would bring over asking to repair.  Although I didn't know what at the time what he was doing, I found the act of him disassembling and struggling to figure what was wrong fascinating as it all seemed like solving one difficult puzzle. Fascinated by the intricacy of electronics, it got me curious to want to know the how and why things worked the way they do.

"Why is the computer so slow?" "Why is the computer screen blue?"  I was never satisfied with my Dad's dismissing replies as I constantly pestered him with questions.  So I discovered many answers for myself by exploring and experimenting.

By High School, I was using the same old desktop running Windows XP that I had as a kid.  Wanting to play the newest games at the time was not an option on that computer, so after saving up a bit of money I finally had enough money to buy something new.  Through the power of the Internet, I was able to learn how to build a desktop that actually ended up working.  Learning today in the Information Age is all possible through the power of technology.  What was once our greatest curiosity can be instantly sated by simply typing into a search engine and looking through the results.  For me, figuring out how the computer works was once one of my greatest mysteries to which although I can read up on it online, I am interested enough to be studying Computer Engineering today.  

To be a well-rounded Computer Engineer, it is important to be knowledgeable in both hardware and software.  I am interested in learning about software engineering and is why I am taking ICS 314 to improve on my skills.  One of my immediate goals by taking this class is to learn javascript and improve my problem-solving skills.  In the future, I plan on working to develop my technical skills (programming, design, industry practices) but in addition soft skills (communication, collaboration, leadership).
